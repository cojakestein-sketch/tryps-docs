## Logline

Jake Stein walks through the streets of New York City and explains how he just fixed travel planning forever. The tone is Dollar Shave Club meets AllState Mayhem — Jake is dry, confident, and cracks a few jokes, but the real comedy comes from what's happening around him. Every person he passes is suffering through the old way of planning a trip. Jake is the only calm one because he has Tryps.

90 seconds. One continuous walk. One pitch. Chaos everywhere except in Jake's pocket.

## The Gag Beats

| # | Beat | What Jake Sees | What Jake Says |
|---|------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | **The Cabo Guys** | A group of guys huddled around one phone, stressed out | *"These guys booked flights to Cabo. That's it. No hotel. Nothing else planned. They leave tomorrow."* |
| 2 | **The Stoop Friends** | Friends sitting on a stoop on a beautiful Saturday, not going anywhere | *"They've been trying to plan a trip to Europe since October."* |
| 3 | **The Nepo Girl** | Girl on the sidewalk yelling at her mom on the phone, trying to get her to plan the trip | Jake walks right past. No words needed. |
| 4 | **The Old-School Traveler** | Someone on a bench, phone on speaker, tinny hold music. Waiting on a travel agent for 45 minutes. | Jake doesn't even need to say anything. The visual says it. |

**NYC Chaos (woven throughout):** Jake almost gets clipped by a cab, rerouted by scaffolding, stared down by a pigeon — and he spins each one into a travel analogy without breaking stride.

## Tone Decision

The original treatment explored an Apple/Terrence Malick direction — quiet, beautiful, restrained. We've pivoted to comedy. The writing has to do heavy lifting. Jake's delivery is the anchor. The gag beats are the engine. The concept works on $5K because it's one person, one walk, one camera, and a script that earns every laugh.

If Rich connects Jake with a comedy director or advisor, Sean stays as director (or creative produces / co-directs alongside). The value is in sharpening the script, blocking, and gag timing — not replacing the creative team.
