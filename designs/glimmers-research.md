## 1. Group Chat Invitation Flows

### Partiful — The Gold Standard for Social Invitations

**What it is:** Partiful treats every event invitation like a tiny act of graphic design. Hosts pick from a library of animated, illustrated, and textured card templates (gradient washes, confetti patterns, retro collage art). The card carries the event name, date, location, and a one-tap RSVP button. When shared via iMessage, the Open Graph preview renders as a rich link card — the event image fills the bubble, the title appears in bold, and the URL preview says something like "Jake invited you to Rooftop Sunset." It does not look like a generic link. It looks like a personalized postcard dropped into your text thread.

**Why it works:**
- **The card IS the invitation.** There is no separate step. The iMessage preview is the first impression, and it is beautiful enough to stop the scroll. The Peak-End Rule applies: the first moment (seeing the invite) and the last moment (tapping RSVP) are both delightful.
- **Social proof is baked in.** The RSVP page shows who else is going. Seeing three friends already confirmed creates urgency without any copy saying "Don't miss out."
- **Tone is the secret weapon.** Partiful's system-generated copy is cheeky and warm. RSVP confirmations say things like "You're in" rather than "Your registration has been confirmed." The copy sounds like a friend, not a platform.
- **No app required.** The invitation works entirely in the mobile browser. You can RSVP without downloading anything. This removes the single biggest friction point for group invitations.
- **Customization signals effort.** When a host picks a specific card template and writes a custom description, it communicates "I cared enough to make this nice for you" — the same social signal as a handwritten note.

**What makes it shareable:** The card is so visually distinctive that forwarding it to someone else ("should we go to this?") doubles as organic growth. The card format has become a cultural signifier among Gen Z and young millennials — receiving a Partiful link means someone considers you part of their inner circle.

### Luma — Professional Elegance

**What it is:** Luma (formerly lu.ma) serves a different audience — professional events, conferences, meetups. Their event pages are clean, information-dense, and beautiful. The invite link opens to a full-bleed hero image, event title in large serif typography, and an immediate "Register" button. The visual hierarchy is tight: image, title, date/time, host, register.

**Why it works:**
- **The event page IS the brand.** Hosts can customize colors, images, and descriptions. The page looks like a micro-website, not a form.
- **Calendar integration is instant.** After RSVP, the event drops into your calendar with one tap. The confirmation email is clean and functional.
- **Discovery feeds attendance.** Luma surfaces "events near you" and "events your connections are attending," turning each event into a node in a social graph.

### Best Practices for SMS/iMessage Invitations That Feel Personal

1. **Rich link previews are non-negotiable.** The Open Graph metadata (og:image, og:title, og:description) must be crafted as carefully as the event page itself. The iMessage bubble IS the invitation for 80% of recipients.

2. **The sender's name should appear in the preview.** "Jake invited you to..." is warm. "Event: Weekend Getaway" is cold. The human name transforms a link into a personal gesture.

3. **One action, one tap.** The link should open directly to the RSVP page, not a homepage, not a login screen, not an app store listing. Every extra tap loses 20-30% of recipients.

4. **Copy should sound texted, not emailed.** Short, lowercase-friendly, conversational. "you in?" energy rather than "You are cordially invited" energy.

5. **The visual should be unique per event.** Generic template = "mass invite." Custom image or host-chosen theme = "you specifically were invited to this specific thing."

**Tryps adaptation:** The trip invitation link in iMessage should render as a warm, textured card with the trip destination photo (or a film-grain default), the trip name, and the host's name. The preview text should read "[Name] invited you to [Trip Name]." When tapped, it opens directly to the trip page with a single "Join" button. No login wall. No app download requirement for the first interaction.

## 2. First-Time App Download / Onboarding

### Duolingo — Value Before Commitment

**What it is:** Duolingo's onboarding is widely considered the gold standard in consumer app design. The critical insight: they let you DO the thing before asking you to BE a member.

**The flow:**
1. **Language selection** — not "what language do you want to learn?" but a visual grid of flags. One tap. No typing.
2. **Goal setting** — "How much time can you spend per day?" with four options (5, 10, 15, 20 min). This is persona-based onboarding disguised as a simple question.
3. **Proficiency check** — "How much [language] do you know?" Three options (New, Some, Lots). No ego threat because all options are valid.
4. **Immediate lesson** — Before creating an account, you complete a real translation exercise. You match words, you hear pronunciation, you get green checkmarks and celebration animations. You FEEL competent.
5. **Account creation** — Only AFTER you have experienced success. The psychological hook is set: you already have progress, and creating an account "saves" it. The Zeigarnik Effect (people remember incomplete tasks) and the Endowment Effect (people value what they feel they already own) work in tandem.
6. **Progress bar** — Visible throughout, showing you are 60% through setup. Goal-Gradient Effect: the closer you are to completion, the faster you move.

**Why it works:**
- **Deferred registration.** Asking for email/password before demonstrating value is like asking someone to sign a lease before seeing the apartment. Duolingo shows you the apartment first.
- **Completion bias.** Once you start the first exercise, quitting feels like wasting effort. The sunk-cost fallacy works FOR the user here because the exercise is genuinely fun.
- **Celebration is immediate and visceral.** Confetti, sound effects, green checkmarks, XP counters. The dopamine hit arrives within 90 seconds of opening the app.
- **No blank slate.** The app never presents an empty screen. Every state has content, a prompt, or a next action.

### Headspace — Calming Authority

**What it is:** Headspace onboards with the same philosophy as their product: calm, clear, unhurried. The first screen asks "What kind of headspace are you looking for?" and presents six goals (stress, sleep, anxiety, focus, relationships, personal growth). Each has a warm illustration.

**Why it works:**
- **The onboarding IS the product experience.** If onboarding for a meditation app felt frantic or demanding, it would undermine the brand promise. Headspace practices what it preaches.
- **Warm illustration over photography.** Their distinctive blob-like characters and hand-drawn aesthetic create a world that feels safe, non-judgmental, and slightly playful. This is harder to achieve with photos.
- **Color palette does emotional work.** Soft beiges, calming blues, warm yellows. The colors say "relax" before a single word of copy appears.
- **Goal-first, not feature-first.** They ask "what do you want?" not "here's what we offer." This reframes the app as a tool in service of the user's existing desire.

### Calm — Sensory Immersion

**What it is:** Calm opens with nature sounds and a full-screen landscape (rain on leaves, mountain lake, crackling fire). Before any text appears, you HEAR the product. The onboarding is minimal: select a few topics, start your first session.

**Why it works:**
- **Multi-sensory first impression.** Most apps are visual-only. Calm uses audio immediately to differentiate and create a physical response (shoulders drop, breathing slows). The medium IS the message.
- **Minimal cognitive load.** Very few choices, very large tap targets, very little text. The onboarding mirrors the product's philosophy: do less, feel more.

### Hinge — Personality-First Identity Building

**What it is:** Hinge's onboarding is fundamentally different from other dating apps. Instead of photo-first (Tinder) or questionnaire-first (eHarmony), Hinge builds your profile around "Prompts" — open-ended questions like "I'm looking for someone who..." or "My most controversial opinion is..." You select prompts from a curated list and write responses. Voice prompts let you record audio answers.

**Why it works:**
- **Self-expression over self-description.** Answering "What's your most irrational fear?" reveals personality in a way that "height, job title, education" never can. The prompts feel like conversation starters, not form fields.
- **The profile IS the onboarding.** Building your Hinge profile feels creative and slightly fun, not administrative. Each prompt chosen is a small act of self-expression. Profiles with voice prompts are 32% more likely to lead to a date because they add humanity.
- **Designed to be deleted.** Hinge's tagline ("the dating app designed to be deleted") signals a finish line. This counterintuitively increases engagement because it promises the experience is temporary and purposeful.
- **Authenticity tools.** Their AI-powered "Prompt Feedback" helps users expand responses while preserving their voice — it nudges, it does not rewrite. This treats users as capable adults who might just need a gentle push.
- **Dating Intentions.** Users can declare what they are looking for (long-term, short-term, figuring it out). 83% of millennial users check this field, suggesting people crave clarity.

### Best Practices for "You've Been Invited" Onboarding

When someone downloads an app because a friend sent them a link (not because they discovered it organically), the onboarding rules change:

1. **Context is already provided.** The user knows what the app does — their friend told them. Skip the "welcome to [App], here's what we do" slides entirely.

2. **Connect them to the reason they came.** If they tapped a trip invitation link, the first screen after download should be THAT trip, with the friend's name visible. "Jake invited you to Bali 2026" — not a generic welcome screen.

3. **Minimize identity friction.** "Sign in with Apple" or "Continue with Phone Number" — two buttons, done. Every additional field (name, birthday, profile photo) can come later. The goal is to get them INTO the trip they were invited to within 30 seconds of download.

4. **Social proof is built in.** Show the other people already in the trip. Faces of real friends are the most powerful onboarding tool possible — they say "you belong here" without any copy.

5. **Defer everything deferrable.** Profile photo? Later. Notification permissions? After they have seen value. Travel preferences? After they have engaged with the trip. The first session should have exactly one goal: join the trip.

**Tryps adaptation:** When someone taps a trip invite link and downloads the app, the post-download screen should show: the trip card (destination photo with film-grain texture, trip name, dates), the host's name and photo, and a list of who is already in. One button: "Join [Trip Name]." Account creation is Apple Sign-In or phone number. Everything else (Travel DNA, profile, notifications) waits.

## 3. Voting / Polling Interfaces

### Instagram Story Polls — Effortless Binary Choice

**What it is:** Instagram polls in Stories present a question with two options (customizable text) as a floating sticker on top of photo/video content. Users tap one side. Results appear instantly as a percentage bar. The whole interaction takes under 2 seconds.

**Why it works:**
- **Binary choice eliminates decision fatigue.** Two options, one tap. Hick's Law in action: fewer choices = faster decisions.
- **The visual context IS the question.** A photo of two restaurants with "Left or Right?" does more work than a text-only poll ever could.
- **Instant feedback loop.** After voting, you see the percentage tilt in real-time. This creates a tiny thrill — were you in the majority? Minority? The result feels like a mini-reveal.
- **Ephemeral = low stakes.** Stories disappear in 24 hours. This makes voting feel casual and consequence-free, which increases participation.
- **Voting is passive content consumption.** Users do not navigate to a "poll section." The poll appears in their existing story-viewing flow. Voting is one tap that does not interrupt what they are already doing.

### Twitter/X Polls — Community Pulse

**What it is:** Text-based polls embedded in tweets. Up to four options. Results shown as horizontal bars with percentages. Total vote count and time remaining are visible.

**Why it works:**
- **Public vote counts create momentum.** Seeing "45,000 votes" signals "this matters" and reduces hesitation about participating.
- **Time pressure drives action.** A countdown timer (24 hours, 7 days) creates urgency without being pushy.
- **Results as content.** The final results become their own conversation piece — people screenshot and share results, which drives secondary engagement.

### Slack Polls (via Polly/Simple Poll)

**What it is:** In-channel polls where each option is a button or emoji reaction. Users click one button without leaving the conversation.

**Why it works:**
- **Zero context switching.** The poll is IN the conversation where the decision is being discussed. You do not open a separate app, browser, or form.
- **Visible participation.** Each person's vote (or their avatar) appears next to the option, creating social pressure to participate and transparency about who has voted.

### Doodle — Scheduling as Group Consensus

**What it is:** Doodle presents a grid of time slots. Each participant marks "yes," "if need be," or "no" for each slot. The slot with the most green marks wins. The interface resembles a spreadsheet, but a friendly one.

**Why it works:**
- **The visualization IS the decision.** You do not need someone to count votes or announce a winner. The most-green column is self-evidently the answer.
- **Three-state voting (yes/maybe/no) captures nuance.** Binary yes/no misses "I could make it work" — which is often the deciding information for group scheduling.
- **Eliminates the back-and-forth.** The entire value proposition is replacing 47 messages in the group chat with one shared grid.

### Best Practices for Group Decision-Making UIs

1. **Make the default action easy, the non-default action possible.** One-tap for the obvious choice, extra taps for the nuanced response.

2. **Show real-time participation status.** "3 of 6 people have voted" creates gentle social pressure and signals progress. People want to be part of the completed thing, not the person holding it up.

3. **Visual results, not numerical results.** A colored bar filling up is instantly legible. A number ("3 votes") requires mental math ("out of how many?"). Use relative visualization, not absolute counts.

4. **Make voting feel like participation, not obligation.** The tone should be "what do you think?" not "please complete this form." Casual language, playful animations, and low visual weight all contribute.

5. **Celebrate consensus.** When a decision is reached, mark the moment. A subtle animation, a confetti micro-interaction, a "You all agreed on..." summary card. The moment of group alignment is emotionally satisfying — do not let it pass silently.

6. **Allow low-friction opinion changes.** People change their minds. If changing a vote requires the same effort as casting it (one tap), participation stays high. If it requires finding a settings page, people will not bother and the data will be stale.

**Tryps adaptation:** Trip voting (restaurants, dates, activities) should use large visual cards with photos — not text lists. One tap to vote. Real-time tally visible as a subtle progress bar on each card. The winning option gets a warm celebration moment ("Your group picked Sunset Cliffs"). Voting should feel like swiping through a curated magazine, not filling out a survey. Use the Instagram playbook: visual-first, binary-or-simple choice, instant feedback, ephemeral energy.

## 4. Travel Personality Quiz UIs

### BuzzFeed Quizzes (Elevated) — Identity as Entertainment

**What it is:** BuzzFeed quizzes ("Which city should you actually live in?", "What kind of traveler are you?") present 8-12 questions with image-based answer options. Each question is a full-screen card. The result is a shareable personality type with a description.

**Why it works:**
- **Every answer is self-expression.** The questions are not "what is the capital of France?" — they are "pick the breakfast that speaks to you." There are no wrong answers because every option reflects taste, not knowledge.
- **Image-first options.** Four photos of different beach scenes is more engaging than four text descriptions of beach preferences. The visual cortex processes faster than the language center.
- **The result is the reward.** People take the quiz because they want to see what they get. The result should feel like a personalized mirror — "yes, that IS me" — not a generic label.
- **Shareability is engineered.** The result card is designed to be screenshotted and texted to friends. "I got Adventurous Explorer, what did you get?" turns a solo experience into a social one.

### Hinge Prompts — Self-Expression Through Constrained Choice

**What it is:** Instead of open-ended "tell us about yourself," Hinge offers a curated list of prompts (questions/sentence starters). You choose which ones to answer, then write your response. The constraint of the prompt paradoxically enables more authentic expression.

**Why it works:**
- **Blank pages are terrifying; prompts are inviting.** "Tell us about yourself" creates anxiety. "My most controversial food opinion is..." creates laughter. The prompt does the heavy lifting of setting tone.
- **Curation signals taste.** WHICH prompts you choose reveals personality as much as the answers themselves. Picking "My most irrational fear" vs "My ideal Sunday" says something before you write a word.
- **Short-form responses feel achievable.** Nobody wants to write a bio. Everyone can finish the sentence "I'm looking for someone who..."

### "This or That" Swipe Interfaces

**What it is:** Two options presented side by side or stacked. Swipe left or right (or tap). Beach or Mountain? Plan Everything or Wing It? Solo or Group? The answers build a profile.

**Why it works:**
- **Binary choice is cognitively effortless.** No ranking, no scales, no text input. The brain can process "this or that" almost instantly.
- **Swipe mechanics feel native to mobile.** The gesture is instinctive for anyone who has used Tinder, Instagram, or TikTok. The interaction is literally playful — it is the same motion as browsing content.
- **Momentum builds through speed.** Quick swipes create a rhythm. 10 questions at 2 seconds each = 20 seconds total. It feels like a game, not a questionnaire.
- **The contrast reveals preference.** "Do you like beaches?" is vague. "Beach or Mountain?" forces a choice that actually means something. The constraint is the feature.

### How to Make a Quiz Feel Like Self-Expression, Not a Test

1. **Never use the word "quiz" in the UI.** Call it "Your Travel DNA," "Discover Your Style," or simply nothing at all — just start asking. The word "quiz" triggers test anxiety.

2. **All options should be aspirational.** Nobody wants to discover they are a "Budget Traveler" (sounds cheap) or "Tourist" (sounds uncultured). Every result should be a compliment: "The Wanderer," "The Curator," "The Adventurer."

3. **Use images and textures, not text and radio buttons.** A form says "we need data from you." A visual experience says "tell us who you are." Full-bleed photos, warm color palettes, and card-based layouts transform data collection into self-discovery.

4. **Progress should feel playful, not administrative.** Instead of "Question 3 of 10," use a visual progress indicator — dots filling in, a path being drawn, a passport being stamped. The metaphor matters.

5. **The reveal moment is the climax.** When the result appears, it should feel like unwrapping a gift. A brief loading moment (even if artificial) builds anticipation. The result card should be visually rich, shareable, and personal. "You're The Curator: You'd rather find one perfect hidden restaurant than eat at ten tourist traps." The user should think "yes, that's exactly right."

6. **Allow contestability.** "Not quite right? Retake" — this gives people agency and signals that the quiz respects their self-knowledge more than its own algorithm.

**Tryps adaptation:** Travel DNA should feel like flipping through a curated photo book, not filling out a form. Each question is a full-bleed image pair (beach sunset vs mountain dawn). Swipe or tap. 8 questions, 30 seconds total. The result is a Travel DNA card with a personality name, a one-line description, and a warm illustration. The card uses film-grain texture and Tryps Red accents. It is designed to be screenshotted and sent to the group chat: "I got The Curator, what are you?"

## 5. Tooltip / Teaching Moment Patterns

### The Spectrum of Teaching Patterns

There are three primary approaches, ranging from least to most intrusive:

**Progressive Disclosure (Best):** Features reveal themselves at the moment they become relevant. You do not see the "split bill" tooltip until someone adds an expense. You do not see the "voting" tutorial until a poll is created. The teaching happens in context, at the point of need.

**Coach Marks / Instructional Overlays (Middle):** A semi-transparent overlay highlights specific UI elements with brief explanatory text. Typically appears on first visit to a screen. Users can dismiss with a tap. Better than slide decks because they are contextual — the user can see the real interface beneath the overlay.

**Deck-of-Cards Welcome Tour (Worst):** Three to five full-screen slides explaining features before the user has seen the app. Users swipe through without reading. Nielsen Norman Group explicitly recommends against this pattern: it "makes simple interfaces seem complex" and "strains memory" because users cannot retain instructions for features they have not yet encountered.

### What the Research Says (Nielsen Norman Group)

- **"The best onboarding is no onboarding."** If the interface requires explanation, the first fix should be redesigning the interface, not adding tutorials.
- **Tooltips should never contain critical information.** If something is essential to task completion, it should be visible on-screen at all times, not hidden behind a hover or tap.
- **Keep tooltip content brief and self-sufficient.** It is microcopy — one sentence, clear benefit, no jargon.
- **Be consistent.** If some icons have tooltips and others do not, users cannot build a mental model of where help lives.
- **Progressive disclosure should not exceed two levels.** Primary view -> secondary detail is fine. Primary -> secondary -> tertiary causes users to get lost.

### Apps That Do Teaching Moments Beautifully

**Figma:**
- Uses inline contextual tips that appear when you hover near a tool for the first time.
- Tips are short (one sentence), visually subtle (small popover, not a modal), and dismissible.
- Advanced features are hidden behind progressive disclosure — the toolbar shows basics, right-click reveals advanced options, keyboard shortcuts are mentioned in tooltips.
- The philosophy: let people start creating immediately, teach refinements as they explore.

**Notion:**
- Uses empty-state education: when a database is empty, the placeholder text explains what could go there and how to start.
- The slash-command menu is self-documenting — typing "/" reveals all available blocks with descriptions.
- Templates serve as implicit tutorials: opening a "Meeting Notes" template teaches the user how Notion works by example, not by instruction.
- The philosophy: show, do not tell. Let the content structure teach the tool.

**Linear:**
- Minimal onboarding. The interface is opinionated and consistent enough that most features are discoverable through convention (keyboard shortcuts follow industry standards, layouts follow expected patterns).
- Uses subtle keyboard shortcut hints in menus — not as tooltips, but as secondary information next to each action.
- The philosophy: respect the user's intelligence. If your UI is well-designed, most things should be self-evident.

**Slack (Slackbot):**
- Uses a character (Slackbot) to deliver teaching moments as conversational messages. "Try typing /remind to set a reminder."
- The teaching comes through the same channel as the product experience (messaging). This feels natural, not overlaid.
- Empty states in new channels include prompts: "This is the beginning of #general. What would you like to say?"

### How to Teach Without Patronizing

1. **Respect the moment.** Only show a teaching element when the user encounters the feature for the first time AND is likely to need help. Never preemptively teach.

2. **One thing at a time.** Coach marks that highlight 5 elements simultaneously ("Here's your profile! Here's notifications! Here's settings!") teach nothing. Highlight one element, explain one concept, then disappear.

3. **Use the product's voice.** If Tryps sounds like a friend in the group chat, the tooltip should too. "Tap here to see who's voted" — not "Utilize the voting dashboard to view participant responses."

4. **Make dismissal effortless.** Tap anywhere to dismiss. Do not make users find a tiny X button. And NEVER show the same tooltip twice after dismissal.

5. **Celebrate discovery, not instruction.** Instead of "Here's how to create a poll," wait until they create their first poll and then say "Nice! Everyone in the trip will get notified." The celebration confirms they did the right thing rather than telling them what to do.

6. **Progressive, not comprehensive.** Teach the first 20% of features that deliver 80% of value. Let power users discover the rest. LinkedIn's research shows that users who receive fewer, more targeted onboarding messages retain better than those who receive exhaustive tutorials.

**Tryps adaptation:** No welcome tour slides. Instead, use contextual micro-tooltips that appear at the moment of relevance. When a user first opens a trip with a pending vote: a warm, small tooltip near the vote cards says "Swipe through to vote on activities." When they add their first expense: "Everyone in the trip can see this. Tryps will handle the math." The tone matches the group chat voice. Tooltips use the warm cream background with Deep Slate text, never a cold blue or gray overlay. Dismiss on tap. Show once, remember forever.

## 6. Trip Card / Event Card Designs

### Partiful Event Cards — THE Reference

**What it is:** Partiful event cards are dense with personality. Each card is a complete visual unit: a textured/illustrated background (chosen by the host from a library of artist-designed templates), the event name in large display type, the date and time, and an RSVP status indicator. The cards are vertical (portrait orientation, roughly 3:4 aspect ratio), image-forward, and designed to look good in three contexts: (1) in-app, (2) as an iMessage link preview, and (3) as a screenshot shared on Instagram or group chats.

**Why it works:**
- **The card is the brand touchpoint.** Every time someone shares an event, they are sharing Partiful's design language. The card is the product's ambassador.
- **Templates are opinionated, not generic.** Instead of letting users upload any image (which leads to blurry photos and bad cropping), Partiful offers curated templates designed by illustrators. This means every card looks professional — the floor is high.
- **Customization within constraints.** Hosts can change the template, edit the text, and add details — but the layout, typography, and visual structure are fixed. This is the Apple approach: freedom within a system.
- **RSVP status as social layer.** The card shows "12 going, 3 maybe" — this turns a static invitation into a living social object. It changes every time someone RSVPs.
- **Animated textures.** Some templates have subtle animations (floating confetti, shimmering gradients). This adds delight without adding information, which is the definition of polish.

### Apple Wallet Passes — The Card as Object

**What it is:** Apple Wallet passes (boarding passes, event tickets, loyalty cards) use a skeuomorphic card metaphor. Each pass has a header strip with a logo, a body with structured data (barcode, seat number, gate), and a distinctive color band. Passes stack in the wallet like physical cards, with slight parallax as you scroll through them.

**Why it works:**
- **Tangibility in a digital space.** The card metaphor works because humans have a lifetime of experience with physical cards. The rounded corners, the drop shadow, the stack — all communicate "this is a thing you possess."
- **Information hierarchy is ruthless.** The most important data (boarding time, seat) is largest. Secondary data (gate, terminal) is smaller. Tertiary data (confirmation number) is smallest. Every pass answers "what do I need to know RIGHT NOW?" at a glance.
- **The card IS the ticket.** At the airport, you hold your phone to the scanner. The pass is not a representation of a ticket — it IS the ticket. This elevates the card from decoration to artifact.
- **Contextual surfacing.** Wallet passes appear on the lock screen when you arrive at the venue (via geofencing) or when the event time approaches. The card finds YOU rather than you finding it.

### Luma Event Cards — Clean Professional Beauty

**What it is:** Luma event cards feature full-bleed hero images, clean sans-serif typography, and a minimal information layout (title, date, host, attendee count). The card serves as both a preview and a landing page.

**Why it works:**
- **Photographic quality sets the tone.** Luma events often use high-quality venue or speaker photos. The image does the atmospheric work.
- **Attendee counts as credibility.** "342 registered" immediately signals "this is real and significant."
- **Host branding.** The host's logo or avatar appears prominently, lending their credibility to the event.

### How to Make a Card So Beautiful It Becomes a Flex to Share

1. **Fixed layout, variable content.** The user chooses what goes on the card (trip name, dates, photo). The designer controls how it looks (typography, spacing, color, texture). This is how Partiful ensures every card looks good — the system prevents bad design.

2. **A single dominant visual.** One large photo or illustration, not a collage. The card should register as one image from across the room (or at thumbnail size in a text thread).

3. **Type hierarchy in three levels max.** Title (large, bold), subtitle (date/location, medium), metadata (attendee count, small). Every additional level of hierarchy makes the card feel more like a flyer and less like an artifact.

4. **Texture and materiality.** Film grain, paper texture, subtle noise, warm shadows — these create the impression that the card exists in the physical world. This is what separates a "card" from a "rectangle with text on it."

5. **Signature color moment.** One color should dominate and be memorable. Partiful uses event-template-specific palettes. Apple Wallet uses the brand color of the issuing company. For Tryps, this is Tryps Red or the warm cream — the card should be instantly recognizable as a Tryps card.

6. **The card should contain a social layer.** Attendee avatars (small circles showing who else is in the trip) transform the card from an information object into a social object. It is not just "Bali Trip" — it is "Bali Trip with [3 friends' faces]."

7. **Design for the screenshot.** The card will be screenshotted and sent in group chats, posted on Instagram stories, forwarded to partners and friends. Design it at the resolution and aspect ratio of a phone screenshot. Test it as a small iMessage preview AND a full-screen object.

**Tryps adaptation:** The Trip Card should be the single most polished design element in the app. It is a vertical card (roughly 9:16 or 3:4) with:
- A full-bleed destination photo with film-grain texture overlay (the disposable camera motif)
- Trip name in Plus Jakarta Sans Bold, large, bottom-aligned with a warm gradient text shadow
- Dates in Space Mono (the numeric font)
- A row of attendee avatar circles (max 5 + "+3 more")
- A subtle Tryps logo watermark (small, bottom corner — like a film camera date stamp)
- Warm cream or deep slate card border depending on light/dark mode
- The entire card should feel like a photo pulled from a disposable camera that also happens to contain trip information
- When shared via iMessage, the Open Graph preview should render the card as a rich image with "[Host] invited you to [Trip]" as the text

## Cross-Cutting Principles: What Connects All Six Categories

### The Feeling Framework

Every glimmer above succeeds because it creates a specific emotional response:

| Category | Primary Feeling | Secondary Feeling |
|----------|----------------|-------------------|
| Invitation Flow | "I'm wanted" | "This looks fun" |
| Onboarding | "I can do this" | "This gets me" |
| Voting/Polling | "My opinion matters" | "This was easy" |
| Travel Quiz | "This is who I am" | "I want to share this" |
| Tooltips | "I just learned something" | "That was helpful, not annoying" |
| Trip Card | "I'm going on a trip" | "I want everyone to know" |

### Universal Patterns

1. **Value before commitment.** Show the thing before asking for the thing. Show the trip before asking for a login. Show the quiz result before asking for an account. Show the vote results before asking for notifications.

2. **Social proof as design element.** Friend avatars, participant counts, and "who's going" lists do more persuasive work than any copy ever could. Faces of real friends are the strongest signal in consumer product design.

3. **Constrained customization.** Let users express themselves within a designed system. Do not give them a blank canvas (they will make something ugly). Do not give them zero options (they will feel no ownership). The sweet spot is "choose from these 12 beautiful templates."

4. **Micro-celebrations.** The tiny confetti, the subtle haptic, the green checkmark, the "You're in!" confirmation. These moments cost almost nothing to build and contribute disproportionately to how the product FEELS.

5. **Group chat energy.** Across every category, the products that win in 2026 sound like your friend texting you, not a corporation emailing you. Short copy. Lowercase OK. Warmth over formality. "you in?" over "Please confirm your attendance."

6. **The screenshot test.** If a screen is not worth screenshotting and sending to a friend, it is not a glimmer. Every hero screen in the brand book should pass this test: would someone voluntarily share this?

### Relevant UX Laws (from Laws of UX)

- **Peak-End Rule:** Users judge an experience by its most intense moment and its ending. Design the invitation receipt, the quiz reveal, and the trip confirmation as peak moments.
- **Aesthetic-Usability Effect:** Users perceive attractive designs as more usable. The Trip Card's beauty is not superficial — it makes the entire app feel more trustworthy.
- **Hick's Law:** Reduce choices at decision points. Two options in a poll, one button on an invite, binary swipes in a quiz.
- **Goal-Gradient Effect:** People accelerate toward completion. Progress bars in onboarding, vote tallies approaching consensus.
- **Zeigarnik Effect:** Incomplete tasks stick in memory. Duolingo's "you have unsaved progress" is precisely this.
- **Von Restorff Effect:** The distinctive item is remembered. The Trip Card should look like nothing else on the user's phone.
