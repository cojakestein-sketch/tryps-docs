Special Report: The State of Peptides

A science-based briefing on BPC-157, the "Wolverine Peptide," and why everyone is losing their minds. March 2026.

What Are Peptides?

Peptides are short chains of amino acids (2-50 amino acids) linked by peptide bonds. Smaller than proteins, they bind to specific cell-surface receptors and trigger intracellular signaling cascades -- mimicking or modulating naturally occurring hormones, neurotransmitters, and growth factors. The body produces thousands of endogenous peptides that regulate appetite, immune response, tissue repair, and more. Pharmacologically, they're interesting because of high specificity and potency at low concentrations, with generally lower toxicity than small-molecule drugs. The catch: most are rapidly degraded by gut enzymes, which is why most require injection.

BPC-157: The Main Character

Body Protection Compound-157 is a synthetic 15-amino-acid peptide derived from a protein found in human gastric juice. First isolated in 1993 by Predrag Sikiric at the University of Zagreb, Croatia. It's classified as a "stable gastric pentadecapeptide" because of its unusual resistance to enzymatic degradation.

Mechanism of action (based on preclinical research):

  • Angiogenesis: Promotes new blood vessel formation by upregulating VEGFR2 and activating the PI3K-Akt-eNOS signaling axis. A 2020 Nature Scientific Reports paper showed it also activates a VEGF-independent pathway via Src-Caveolin-1-eNOS.
  • Nitric oxide modulation: Bidirectional -- promotes beneficial NO-mediated vasodilation while counteracting cytotoxic NO effects.
  • Growth factor modulation: Interacts with EGF, bFGF, VEGF, HGF. Upregulates receptor expression. Engages ERK1/2 signaling and promotes fibroblast activity.
  • Gut-brain axis: Acts bidirectionally -- anti-ulcer and cytoprotective in the GI tract, plus dopaminergic, serotonergic, GABAergic, and opioid system interactions in the CNS.
  • Anti-inflammatory: Reduces inflammatory markers and stabilizes intestinal permeability.

The animal data is genuinely extensive (~178 publications on PubMed). Rats showed accelerated healing of transected Achilles tendons, gut mucosa, peripheral nerves, and traumatic brain injury. BPC-157 protected against NSAID-induced damage, colitis, and even electrolyte overdose.

The massive gap: As of March 2026, only 3 pilot studies have been published in humans -- totaling fewer than 30 patients. No completed randomized controlled trials. No Phase I trials for the musculoskeletal indications being marketed. A 2025 systematic review in the American Journal of Sports Medicine screened 544 articles: of 36 that met criteria, 35 were animal studies. The single clinical study was a 12-person case series with no control group.

The "Wolverine Peptide" and Sleep Claims

The "Wolverine" nickname comes from accelerated tissue repair in animal models -- tendons, ligaments, muscles, bones, gut, nerves, and blood vessels all healed faster in rats. The Marvel analogy caught fire in biohacker communities. Some clinics now market a "Wolverine Stack" (BPC-157 + TB-500).

Sleep/REM claims: There is no published evidence -- not even preclinical -- linking BPC-157 to sleep or REM. This is likely conflation with DSIP (Delta Sleep-Inducing Peptide), an entirely different compound studied since the 1970s, or with growth hormone secretagogues like CJC-1295/Sermorelin that users claim improve deep sleep. Users experiencing better sleep on BPC-157 are likely benefiting from reduced pain (less pain = better sleep), which then gets attributed to the peptide itself.

Why the Medical Community Is Cautious

  • Translation failure rate: 85-95% of drugs that work in animals fail in human trials. BPC-157 has never been subjected to this gauntlet.
  • No pharmacokinetics in humans: We don't know how BPC-157 is absorbed, distributed, metabolized, or excreted in humans. The half-life, dose-response curve, drug interactions -- all unknown. The 2025 IV pilot (2 adults, single dose) is the entirety of our PK data.
  • Cancer risk (the big one): BPC-157 is a potent angiogenesis promoter -- it upregulates VEGFR2 and stimulates blood vessel formation. These are the same pathways tumors exploit to grow blood supply. No study has investigated whether BPC-157 could accelerate existing cancers. As a 2025 narrative review noted: "Pathways promoting healing can be hijacked by cancer cells."
  • Contamination: Independent testing found 30% of online peptides contained incorrect amino acid sequences and 65% had endotoxin levels above safety thresholds. Case reports describe severe infections from contaminated injectable peptides.
  • Zero long-term safety data. No study has followed BPC-157 users for more than 12 months.

The Regulatory War (2024-2026)

FDA crackdown: In late 2023, BPC-157 was placed on the FDA's Category 2 list ("may present significant safety risks"), banning it from compounding pharmacies. In September 2025 alone, 50+ warning letters went to peptide vendors. WADA, NCAA, NFL, NBA, MLB, NHL, UFC, and PGA have all banned it.

The RFK Jr. reversal: On the Joe Rogan Experience (Feb 27, 2026), HHS Secretary Kennedy announced plans to move ~14 peptides back to Category 1, restoring compounding eligibility. However, as of March 2026, no FDA rule has actually changed -- no Federal Register notice, no statute amendment. Compounding eligibility is not FDA approval. Legal experts describe it as an announcement with no regulatory teeth yet.

The market: Global peptide therapeutics market estimated at $45-50B (2024), driven overwhelmingly by GLP-1 agonists (Novo Nordisk's semaglutide alone generated ~$25B in 2024). The gray-market peptide economy (BPC-157, TB-500, etc.) is estimated at $1-3B, mostly sourced from Chinese manufacturers with minimal quality control.

Bottom Line

The mechanisms are real. The animal data is voluminous. The human data is almost nonexistent.

Peptides like semaglutide and tirzepatide prove the category works -- when properly validated through rigorous clinical trials. BPC-157 has never been subjected to that process. The gap between "rats healed faster in a Croatian lab" and "you should inject this" is approximately a decade of research and billions of dollars that no one has spent. The hype is being driven by a perfect storm: Joe Rogan's megaphone, RFK Jr.'s political promises, biohacker culture's frustration with pharma timelines, and the legitimizing halo of the GLP-1 revolution. What's missing is the science. 178 papers, 80% from one lab, fewer than 30 humans ever studied.

What to watch: Whether the RFK Jr. reclassification actually materializes in the Federal Register. Whether any institution launches a proper Phase I/II trial for BPC-157 musculoskeletal indications. And the next-generation pipeline: oral peptide delivery (avoiding injections), AI-designed peptides, and retatrutide (Eli Lilly's triple-agonist that produced 24% weight loss in Phase 2).

4. Deep Dive: First-Response Classification for the Tryps Agent

Topic source: Jake's brain dump from earlier today — spec out a study of the first messages people send to the Tryps agent. No topic-suggestion DM response found, so using Jake's own direction.

What It Is

First-response classification is the practice of categorizing the very first message a user sends to a conversational agent, then using those categories to optimize the agent's behavior for the most common patterns. For Tryps, this means analyzing what real users say when they first encounter the trip-planning agent via iMessage — do they send a destination? A date? Do they mess with it? Are they confused?

Why It Matters Now

The iMessage agent is Tryps' primary growth surface. The first interaction determines whether a user converts from "someone who received a link" to "someone who plans a trip." Jake's insight is right: if you understand the distribution of first messages (60% might be "Vegas 2026", 15% might be confused, 10% might be testing boundaries), you can optimize the agent's response for each pattern — and you can test coverage systematically instead of guessing.

Key Takeaways

  • Extract first unique messages from iMessage logs. Jake's iMessages are the best source. Deduplicate by user. Target first ~100 unique messages for the initial classification round.
  • Use Claude to generate the taxonomy. Feed the raw messages into Claude with a prompt like: "Classify these into response types. Create a taxonomy that would predict the next 1,000 users." Expected categories: destination+date, destination-only, question about the agent, testing/trolling, confused/accidental, social response ("haha"), planning intent without destination.
  • Project to 1,000 and build the QA matrix. Each category becomes a test row: what happens today vs. what should happen. Rizwan's QA person tests each pattern. Output goes into markdown files in the tripful repo — not tickets.
  • Critical insight: the "I Don't Care-er" signal. 60% of trip-goers are "I Don't Care-ers." Their first message will likely be short, low-effort, or a response to someone else's plan. The agent needs to handle this gracefully — not push them to plan, but make them feel included.
  • Schedule the Jake+Rizwan meeting this week to discuss data capture architecture, markdown generation per conversation, and the QA assignment.

Resource: Chatbot onboarding feedback patterns — dynamic content that adapts to user behavior reduces time-to-competency by 40%.
