However You Get To Us | Tryps Launch Video Treatment

    Tryps Launch Video Treatment
    However You Get To Us.
    A 60-second launch film for Tryps. Every frame is neutral white. The only color in the world is Tryps Red on the device screen. Different people. Different lives. Same result: they text Marty, and their trip gets planned.

      Client
      Tryps

      Director
      Sean DeFaria

      Format
      60s / 30s / 15s

      Launch
      May 2, 2026

      Budget
      $2,500 - $4,000

    Logline
    In a world drained of all color, the only thing that glows red is the screen where you text Marty from Tryps, because it doesn't matter who you are or how you found us, the moment you text him, your trip is real.

    01
    Concept Overview

    The entire world of this video is desaturated. Not black and white. Not gray. Just drained. White walls, cream couches, pale wood, neutral clothing. Everything looks beautiful, clean, and a little bit empty. Like something is missing.

    The only color that exists is Tryps Red (#D9071C) on the device screen. Every time someone opens iMessage and texts Marty, the red bleeds through. It's the only warmth in the frame. The only sign of life. The screen becomes a portal. That red is a promise: you text this person, and your real life starts.

    We cut between five people across five contexts. A college kid on a beat-up laptop. A mom on an iPad at the kitchen table. A couple sharing a phone on the couch. A guy at work sneaking a text on his desktop. A woman alone at a coffee shop on her phone. Different ages, different devices, different reasons. But the same thing happens. They text Marty. He responds immediately. Flights, hotels, activities. Done. The last shot is the only time we see the real world in full color: a plane lifting off through a window. Then the Tryps logo. Then "Go while you can."

    02
    Visual Treatment

    Color Palette
    The world: Completely neutral. Not clinical white, not hospital white. Warm white. Think Apple store meets Scandinavian apartment meets a really nice Airbnb. Cream, off-white, pale oak, light gray fabrics, white walls. Clothing is all neutrals: white tees, beige, oatmeal, gray sweats, denim washed so light it reads as white.
    The device: The ONLY color in the entire video is the iMessage UI tinted Tryps Red. The contact name "Marty from Tryps" in red. The text bubbles. The Tryps logo in the chat. When the screen is visible, it should feel like a hot coal sitting in a snowfield. Impossible to ignore.
    The break: In the final shot, the plane taking off, we let natural color flood back in. Blue sky, warm sunlight. The world wakes up. This is the payoff for 55 seconds of restraint.

    Camera Style
    Locked off. No handheld. Every shot is composed and still. Slight, slow push-ins only (2-3% over 5 seconds, barely perceptible). This is Apple keynote energy, not Casey Neistat energy. The stillness makes the red screen hit harder. The camera is observational, not participatory. We are watching people discover something. We don't move. They do.
    Close-ups of thumbs typing on screens. Over-the-shoulder framings showing the full iMessage thread. Wide shots establishing each environment. The camera treats every scene with the same quiet respect, whether it's a 20-year-old in a dorm or a 55-year-old at a dining table.

    Lighting Direction
    Soft, diffused, directional. Large window light or a big softbox simulating it. Minimal shadows. No hard light, no dramatic contrast. The lighting should make everything feel open, airy, and slightly dreamlike. Like a Sunday morning. We want every environment to feel like a place you'd actually want to be in.
    No overhead fluorescents, no office lighting, no cool tones. Even the "guy at work" scene should feel warm and soft, like a creative studio, not a cubicle farm.

    Art Direction for the White World
    Aspirational, not sterile. This is the critical line. The white world should make you want to live there, not feel like you're in a lab. Plants in white pots. A single book on a table. A mug of coffee (white mug, obviously). Warm textures: a knit blanket, a linen couch, wood grain. The neutrality should feel chosen, not imposed. Like these people have taste, and their taste happens to be minimal.
    Every prop, every surface, every fabric is auditioned for color. If it's not white, cream, beige, gray, or pale wood, it doesn't exist in this world.

    03
    Scene-by-Scene Breakdown
    Total runtime: 58 seconds. Five scenes, one montage beat, one end card.

        Scene 01
        0:00 - 0:10
      
      The College Kid
      Wide shot: a dorm room. White walls, messy bed with white sheets, a beat-up MacBook on a desk. A guy in a white tee sits cross-legged on the bed, laptop open. He's scrolling through a group chat called "SPRING BREAK???" where the last 20 messages are just people saying "I'm down" and "someone figure it out" and "lol." He opens a new iMessage thread.
      Cut to close-up of the screen. He types:
      
        To: Marty from Tryps
        "me and 5 friends want to go somewhere warm for spring break. march 15-20. we're broke lol"
      
      Three dots appear. Marty responds instantly:
      
        Marty from Tryps
        "Say less. Puerto Rico. $340 flights from JFK, beachfront Airbnb $89/night split 6 ways. I'll lock it in. Want me to send the group a link?"
      
      Cut to the kid's face. Half-smile. He types "do it."
      Camera: wide establishing shot, then slow push-in on laptop screen, then mid-close on face. All locked off.

        Scene 02
        0:10 - 0:20
      
      The Mom
      Kitchen. White marble countertop, white cabinets. An iPad propped against a stand. A woman in her late 40s, reading glasses, cream sweater. She's got a notepad next to the iPad with handwritten scribbles: dates, names, "ask Linda about the hotel." She puts the pen down and picks up the iPad.
      Cut to screen. She types carefully, full sentences:
      
        To: Marty from Tryps
        "Hi Marty, I'm planning a family reunion for 12 people in August. We need somewhere with a big house and things for kids to do. Budget is around $400 per family."

        Marty from Tryps
        "Hi! I've got the perfect setup. Lake house in the Catskills, sleeps 14, private dock, 20 min from a water park. $2,200 for the week split 4 families. Want me to send everyone the details?"
      
      She smiles. Closes the notepad. Done.
      Camera: medium shot from across the counter, close-up on iPad screen, reaction shot. Warm window light from the left.

        Scene 03
        0:20 - 0:30
      
      The Couple
      Couch. Two people, mid-20s, sharing a phone. She's holding it, he's leaning over her shoulder. White couch, white blanket draped over the back, pale wood coffee table. They're laughing about something on the screen.
      She opens iMessage and types while he watches:
      
        To: Marty from Tryps
        "anniversary trip. somewhere romantic. we've never been to europe. 5 days in june. surprise me"

        Marty from Tryps
        "Amalfi Coast. Flights through Rome, cliffside hotel in Positano, boat tour, dinner reservation at a spot with no menu. $1,800 each all in. Here's the full itinerary."
      
      He grabs the phone from her. Scrolls through the itinerary. They look at each other.
      Camera: over-the-shoulder two-shot, tight on the phone screen, then a wider shot catching their reaction together. Slow 2% push-in on the two-shot.

        Scene 04
        0:30 - 0:38
      
      The Work Guy
      Studio-style office. White desk, big monitor, wireless keyboard. A guy, early 30s, button-down with sleeves rolled. He glances over his shoulder (making sure nobody's watching), then opens iMessage on his Mac desktop. The iMessage window is the only thing on screen with color.
      
        To: Marty from Tryps
        "bachelor party. 8 guys. vegas or miami. april 10-13. make it stupid"

        Marty from Tryps
        "Miami. Rooftop suite on Collins Ave, day 1 deep sea fishing, day 2 pool party, day 3 golf. $620/person. Want the move?"
      
      He grins. Types "book it" under his desk.
      Camera: wide office shot, tight on desktop iMessage window (red UI pops against the muted office), close-up on his hands typing. Quick cuts, slightly faster pacing than previous scenes.

        Scene 05
        0:38 - 0:46
      
      The Solo Traveler
      Coffee shop. A woman, late 20s, sitting alone at a white marble table. AirPods in. Phone in hand. She's the only person we see who seems like she's done this before. Calm, certain. She doesn't even hesitate.
      
        To: Marty from Tryps
        "just me. tokyo. 10 days. i want to get lost"

        Marty from Tryps
        "10 days in Tokyo solo. Shibuya hotel first 3 nights, ryokan in Hakone for 2, then Kyoto for 5. No tour groups. Just you and a list of places nobody posts about. Ready?"
      
      She locks her phone. Puts it face-down on the table. Takes a sip of coffee. Done.
      Camera: profile shot, close-up on phone screen, then a wide shot of her sitting in the cafe. The phone face-down on the table is the last action before the montage. Hold the wide for a beat.

        Montage
        0:46 - 0:52
      
      The Cascade
      Quick cuts, 1 second each. All five people in the same moment: checking their phones and seeing confirmations. The college kid's group chat exploding. The mom forwarding to her family. The couple screenshotting the itinerary. The work guy texting his boys "it's booked." The solo traveler smiling at a boarding pass notification.
      Every screen still glows red. The rest of the world is still white. But everyone is moving now. Packing. Zipping bags. Grabbing keys. Life is happening.
      Camera: fast cuts, 1-1.5 seconds each. Close-ups on hands, screens, faces. Same locked-off style but the editing creates the energy. No handheld.

        Final Shot
        0:52 - 0:55
      
      The World Wakes Up
      A window. An airplane lifting off against a blue sky. For the first time in the entire video, we see real color. Blue sky. Warm sunlight hitting the wing. The color floods back in like someone turned the saturation dial from 0 to 100. It's overwhelming after 52 seconds of white. You feel it physically.
      This is the moment. The trip is real. The world has color again because you're going somewhere.
      Camera: locked off, slightly low angle through a large window (could be airport terminal, could be apartment window near the flight path). The plane enters frame left, exits frame right. 3-second hold.

    04
    End Card / CTA

      However you get to us.
      Text Marty. Go while you can.

    On screen (0:55 - 0:58): White background. "However you get to us." fades in, set in the brand typeface, centered. Holds for 1.5 seconds.
    On screen (0:58 - 1:00): Tryps logo in red fades in below. Underneath: "Text Marty. Start your trip." and the App Store badge. The tagline "Go while you can." appears last, small, at the bottom. Clean fade to white.
    No URL. No QR code. No social handles. Just the name, the action, and the download. One thing to do.

    05
    Sound Design

    Music
    A single piano note that builds. The video opens with near-silence. Just room tone. Then a simple, sparse piano motif begins around 0:05. Minimal. One or two notes repeating. Think Nils Frahm or Olafur Arnalds. Warm, not sad. Curious, not dramatic.
    The piano builds slowly across the 60 seconds. By the montage beat (0:46), a soft pad or string swell joins underneath. When the plane lifts off and color returns (0:52), the music opens up fully for the first time. A full chord. Not a crescendo, not a drop. Just completion. Like a breath you didn't realize you were holding.
    The music should feel like the score to a Terrence Malick film, not a startup commercial. Restrained, human, and warm.

    Sound Effects
    
      iMessage send/receive sounds: The real ones. Authentic Apple sounds. Every text sent and received should have the actual iMessage "whoosh" and "ding." These are sounds everyone recognizes instinctively. They anchor the video in reality.
      Typing sounds: Soft keyboard taps. Different for each device (laptop keys vs. phone glass vs. iPad glass). Subtle, just enough to feel present.
      Room tone: Each environment has its own quiet atmosphere. The dorm has a faint hum. The kitchen has a clock ticking. The coffee shop has muffled background noise. These are felt, not heard.
      The plane: A low, warm rumble as it enters frame. Not a roar. Just enough to feel the weight of it.

    VO Recommendation: No VO.
    No voiceover. No narration. No text on screen until the end card. The iMessage conversations ARE the script. The viewer reads them in real time, the same way they read their own texts. A voiceover would break the spell. The power of this concept is that it feels like eavesdropping on five private moments, not being sold something.
    The only text that appears outside of the device screens is the end card. Everything before that is shown, not told.

    06
    Platform Cuts

        Hero Version
        60 seconds
        The full treatment as written. All five scenes, montage, plane, end card. 16:9 horizontal for YouTube, website, Product Hunt, and press embeds. This is the definitive version. Shot and edited first. Everything else is derived from this.

        TikTok / Reels
        30 seconds
        9:16 vertical. Three scenes only: College Kid, Couple, Solo Traveler. Faster pacing, tighter cuts. Open on the College Kid typing (no wide establishing shot, start on the phone screen). Skip the montage beat entirely. Go straight from Solo Traveler locking her phone to the plane shot and end card. The texts should be readable in vertical without squinting. Reframe all device close-ups for portrait.

        Pre-Roll
        15 seconds
        One scene only: the Couple. It's the most universally relatable and the most visually dynamic (two people, shared phone, the reaction). Open on the text being typed, show Marty's response, catch their reaction, smash cut to the plane, end card. 15 seconds flat. The red screen needs to be visible within the first 2 seconds.

        X / Twitter
        45 seconds
        16:9 or 1:1 square. Four scenes (drop the Mom for pacing). Slightly tighter edits than the hero. Paired with tweet copy: "You text Marty. He plans your trip. That's it." This version optimizes for the quote-tweet reaction: people sharing it and tagging their friend group.

    What Changes Across Cuts
    Pacing, not content. The iMessage conversations stay the same. The white world stays the same. The red device stays the same. We just trim the number of people and the breathing room between scenes. The 60-second version lets moments land. The 15-second version is a punch. Same fist, different speed.
    The plane and end card appear in every cut. That moment of color returning is the emotional payoff. It cannot be cut from any version.

    08
    Why This Works

    The white world does three things at once. First, it makes the red screen impossible to ignore. Your eye goes straight to the only color in the frame, which is always the product. Second, it makes the video feel premium without spending premium money. White rooms + soft light + still camera = Apple energy on an indie budget. Third, it sets up the emotional payoff: when color returns in the final shot, you feel the relief of the trip becoming real. 55 seconds of restraint earns that 3-second release.

    The iMessage conversations do the selling. We never explain what Tryps is. We never say "AI-powered travel agent." We never list features. We just show five people texting Marty and getting their trip planned in seconds. The viewer fills in the blanks themselves, which is ten times more powerful than being told. Every text Marty sends is a product demo disguised as a conversation.

    The diversity is the concept, not decoration. "However you get to us" means it literally does not matter who you are. College kid on a laptop. Mom on an iPad. Couple sharing a phone. Office worker on a desktop. Solo traveler on an iPhone. Five different demographics, five different devices, five different trip types, one result. The casting IS the thesis.

    It's shareable because it's relatable. Every viewer will see themselves in one of these five people. The college kid planning spring break. The couple who've been saying "we should go somewhere" for months. The person at work who'd rather be anywhere else. The solo traveler who just wants to go. When someone watches this and tags their friend group with "this is us," we've won.
