# Sample Pitch Deck Analysis & Recommended Structure for Tryps

> Synthesized from research on Wispr Flow, Partiful, LINQ, Airbnb, Superhuman, Buffer, Tinder, Front, and canonical VC frameworks (Sequoia, YC, a16z, First Round).
> Generated: 2026-04-05

## Executive Summary

We analyzed 8 company pitch decks/fundraising strategies and 4 VC frameworks. The striking finding: **3 of 5 target companies (Wispr Flow, Partiful, LINQ) never made a public pitch deck.** They raised $81M, $27M, and $28M respectively through product-led conviction — investors came to them. The companies that did use decks (Airbnb, Buffer, Tinder, Superhuman, Front) share a consistent structure: problem-first, proxy traction when pre-launch, one-sentence business model, 10-14 slides.

Tryps sits at the intersection of Airbnb (travel, marketplace, pre-scale), Partiful (group coordination, viral loops, Gen Z), and Wispr Flow (AI-native, agent-first). The recommended deck below draws from all three.

## Company-by-Company Findings

### 1. Wispr Flow — $81M raised, no pitch deck ever made

**Key finding:** Product was the pitch. Every VC was already a daily user of Wispr Flow before fundraising conversations happened. Investors approached them, not the other way around.

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total raised | $81M across 4 rounds |
| Valuation | ~$700M post-money |
| Deck? | None. Ever. |
| Stage at seed | Pre-product (BCI prototype decoding ~10 words from thoughts) |
| Stage at Series A | 50% MoM revenue growth, 20% free-to-paid conversion, 90% organic |

**Fundraising playbook:**
- Build product so good target investors become daily users
- Let product create inbound demand for the round
- Be radically transparent about risks when investors approach
- Only take money from "pre-emptive investors" with independent conviction
- Frame vision as infrastructure ("voice OS"), not a feature ("dictation tool")

**Tanay Kothari's approach:** "I didn't need to convince them about why the product was good. All I had to tell them is — if you believe why Wispr is good today, here is where we can take the company."

**Relevance to Tryps:** The anti-deck strategy works when investors are users. For Tryps, this means: get the iMessage agent in the hands of target investors before pitch meetings. Let them experience a group trip planning session. The demo IS the deck.

### 2. Partiful — $27M raised at $140M valuation, $0 revenue

**Key finding:** Framed as a human connection platform solving loneliness, NOT a "party planning app." Raised $20M Series A1 from a16z at $0 revenue.

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total raised | $27.3M (seed + Series A1) |
| Valuation | ~$140M post-money (A1), rumored $400M later |
| Deck? | No public deck exists |
| Revenue at A1 | $0 |
| Users | 5M total by late 2025, 500K MAU in Q1 2025 |

**What they emphasized to investors:**
- "Loneliness, social isolation, and how social media was dividing us more than ever"
- Parties as fundamental human behavior, not a niche feature
- 1,000%+ event growth, 600% user activity increase, 90% users under 30
- Built-in viral loop: every event SMS-invites non-users who discover the platform
- Capital efficiency: ~20 people built a product used by millions

**How they overcame "it's just a party app" skepticism:**
- Positioned parties as **evergreen human behavior** tied to the loneliness epidemic
- Let organic traction speak — no paid acquisition
- Positioned $0 revenue as a **strength** (user-first, no enshittification)
- a16z invested because: "human connection isn't a nice-to-have — it's essential"

**Direct parallel to Tryps:**

| Dimension | Partiful | Tryps |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Core insight | Social media made us lonely; fix with frictionless IRL gathering | Group travel is broken; fix with an agent in your group chat |
| Distribution | SMS invites create viral loop | iMessage agent creates viral loop |
| Investor objection | "It's just party invites" | "It's just trip planning" |
| Counter | "Parties are evergreen, touching everyone" | "Travel is the largest consumer category, groups are underserved" |
| Revenue | $0 at $140M valuation | Built-in revenue from bookings (structural advantage) |

**Tryps advantage over Partiful:** Built-in transaction revenue from day one via travel bookings. Partiful had to justify $140M at $0 revenue. Tryps can point to Duffel/booking revenue.

### 3. LINQ — $28M raised, pivoted 3x before PMF

**Key finding:** Demand found them. After Poke went viral on their infrastructure, 100+ AI companies came to them. They pivoted from NFC business cards → business messaging API → AI agent infrastructure.

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total raised | ~$28.4M across 5 rounds |
| Latest round | $20M Series A (Feb 2026) from TQ Ventures |
| Deck? | No public deck |
| Key metrics | 295% NRR, zero churn, 132% QoQ growth, 134K MAU, 30M+ msgs/month |

**Reconstructed pitch narrative:**
- **Problem:** AI agents trapped behind app downloads. Consumers have app fatigue.
- **Solution:** Give AI agents a real phone number. They text users via iMessage/RCS/SMS. No app download. Blue bubbles.
- **Why now:** Poke went viral proving the model. iMessage infrastructure newly accessible.
- **Moat:** 90% cheaper than Twilio. SOC 2 Type II. Blue-bubble iMessage (not available through legacy CPaaS).
- **Team:** Three former Shipt founding team members (scaled to $550M Target acquisition).

**Relevance to Tryps:** LINQ is literally Tryps' infrastructure provider. Their thesis — "AI should live in messaging, not in apps" — is Tryps' thesis at the application layer. LINQ proves the pipes work; Tryps proves the product works.

### 4. Airbnb — The legendary 2008 seed deck

**Key finding:** 14 slides, radical simplicity, led with problem not metrics. Raised $600K. Became a $100B company. The most studied pitch deck in startup history.

| Slide # | Slide | Key Content |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Cover | "Book rooms with locals, rather than hotels." (7 words) |
| 2 | Problem | Three bolded pain points: price, disconnection, no easy way to book |
| 3 | Solution | Save money, make money, share culture |
| 4 | Market Validation | 630K CouchSurfing users + 17K Craigslist listings as proxy demand |
| 5 | Market Size | 2B+ trips/year → 560M budget travelers → 84M addressable |
| 6 | Product | Three-step flow with real screenshots |
| 7 | Business Model | "We take a 10% commission on each transaction." (one sentence) |
| 8 | Go-to-Market | Events, partnerships, Craigslist dual-posting hack |
| 9 | Competition | 2x2 chart: Online/Offline vs Affordable/Expensive |
| 10 | Competitive Advantages | First mover, UX, host incentives, verified profiles |
| 11 | Team | Three founders, brief bios |
| 12 | Press | Media logos |
| 13 | Testimonials | Three real customer quotes |
| 14 | The Ask | $500K-$600K for 12-month runway |

**Why it's legendary:**
1. Radical simplicity — every slide understood in <10 seconds
2. The tagline — 7 words, no buzzwords, instant comprehension
3. One-sentence business model — "We take a 10% commission"
4. Used competitor data as proof of demand (CouchSurfing + Craigslist) when they had no traction
5. Customer testimonials compensated for missing metrics
6. Narrative arc: each slide answers the question the previous slide raised

**Key lesson for Tryps:** When you lack traction, anchor against existing behavior. Airbnb pointed to CouchSurfing and Craigslist. Tryps can point to: group chat chaos (screenshots of real group texts), WhatsApp group polls, Google Sheets trip planning, Splitwise for expenses — all proof that group travel coordination demand exists and current tools are terrible.

### 5. Best Pre-Launch Decks — Buffer, Tinder, Superhuman, Front

**Buffer (Seed, $500K, 2011)** — 13 slides. Led with market wave (social media explosion), then features, then traction (55K users, $11K MRR). Radical transparency. Published deck publicly. Lesson: ride a macro trend.

**Tinder/Matchbox (Incubator, 2012)** — 9 slides. Led with a **character named Matt** who's afraid of rejection at parties. Made investors FEEL the problem before showing any product. No traction metrics — just product screenshots and an emotional narrative. Lesson: story beats data at pre-launch.

**Superhuman (Seed, ~$800K, 2014)** — No public deck. Raised on founder credibility (prior exit to LinkedIn) + contrarian thesis ("people will pay for email") + high-fidelity design mockups. No code, no users. Lesson: founder-market fit + visual artifacts that make the future feel real.

**Front (Series A, $10M, 2016)** — 21 slides. $100K MRR, 50% customer spend increase after 12 months. Most data-heavy deck in the set. CEO spent 90% of days perfecting it. Got 3 term sheets in 10 days. Lesson: when you have metrics, lead with them.

## Canonical VC Frameworks

### Sequoia Capital (10 slides)

1. Company Purpose (one declarative sentence)
2. Problem
3. Solution
4. **Why Now** ← Sequoia says this matters more than founders realize
5. Market Size (bottom-up, not top-down)
6. Competition
7. Product
8. Business Model
9. Team
10. Financials / The Ask

### Y Combinator (10-12 slides)

1. Title + one-line description
2. Problem
3. Solution
4. Traction (omit entirely if weak — bad traction slide is worse than none)
5. Market
6. Business Model
7. Team
8. The Ask

**Michael Seibel:** Arrange from most impressive to least impressive, not in template order.

### a16z Consumer Startup Framework

Key evaluation criteria:
- Growth loops (how engagement creates more users)
- Quality of acquisition (organic vs paid)
- DAU/MAU ratio: 25%+ OK, 40%+ Good
- Monthly growth: 20%+ OK, 35%+ Good, 50%+ Great
- Cohort retention curves — do newer cohorts perform better?
- Monetization fit with engagement patterns

## Cross-Deck Pattern Analysis

### What every great pre-launch deck does:

1. **Leads with a problem people can feel.** Tinder used a character. Airbnb used "hotels are expensive and impersonal." Partiful used loneliness. The problem is visceral in the first 30 seconds.

2. **Anchors against existing behavior.** Nobody said "we're creating a new category." Airbnb pointed to Craigslist. Buffer pointed to social media growth. The message: people already do this, badly.

3. **Replaces traction with conviction signals.** Superhuman used designs + founder credibility. Tinder used product screenshots + story. Airbnb used Craigslist volume as proxy traction. Partiful pointed to post-COVID loneliness data.

4. **States the business model in one sentence.** Airbnb: "10% commission." Buffer: "Freemium." If it takes a paragraph, it's too complex.

5. **Makes the ask explicit.** How much, what milestones it unlocks. Most failed decks forget to actually ask.

6. **Keeps design clean, not flashy.** Every deck here is visually simple. DocSend confirms: investors spend the most time on team and financials, not design.

7. **10-14 slides is the sweet spot.** Airbnb: 14. Buffer: 13. Tinder: 9. Sequoia recommends 10. YC says 10-12.

### The "no deck" pattern (Wispr, Partiful, LINQ):

Three of five companies we researched never made a traditional deck. They shared:
- Product so good it created inbound investor interest
- Investors were users before they were investors
- Radical transparency when conversations happened
- Vision framed as infrastructure/platform, not feature

## Recommended Slide Structure for Tryps

Based on all patterns above, optimized for Tryps' exact position: pre-launch, working demo, 11-person team, travel + consumer social + AI agent.

### 12-Slide Deck

| # | Slide | Content | Inspiration |
|---|-------|---------|-------------|
| 1 | **Cover** | "Tryps — A travel agent in your iMessage group chat." One sentence. Logo. | Airbnb's 7-word tagline |
| 2 | **The Problem** | Screenshot of a real group chat devolving into chaos trying to plan a trip. "Group travel is a $900B market coordinated in group chats with zero tools." Three pain points: (1) Nobody decides, (2) Nobody books, (3) Nobody pays back. | Airbnb's 3 bolded pain points, Tinder's emotional hook |
| 3 | **The Insight** | "The group chat is where travel decisions already happen. We put an agent inside it." Behavioral anchor: X billion group messages about travel per year. Screenshots of Splitwise, Google Sheets, WhatsApp polls — current broken tools. | Airbnb's market validation via existing behavior |
| 4 | **The Solution** | Live demo / walkthrough: Text Tryps → agent joins group → polls the group → suggests itinerary → books flights → splits costs. Show real iMessage screenshots. | Airbnb's 3-step product flow |
| 5 | **How It Works** | Architecture in plain English: iMessage agent + mobile app + voice calls + booking pipeline (Duffel flights, hotels, activities). Show the product stack without jargon. | Front's product grounding |
| 6 | **Why Now** | Three converging forces: (1) LLMs make conversational agents possible, (2) iMessage/RCS APIs newly accessible (LINQ infrastructure), (3) Post-COVID travel boom + group travel growing fastest. | Sequoia's "Why Now" emphasis |
| 7 | **Market** | Bottom-up: $900B global travel market → group trips (35-40% of leisure travel) → digitally-booked group trips → Tryps' addressable segment. Commission on bookings = built-in revenue. | Airbnb's TAM/SAM/SOM funnel |
| 8 | **Business Model** | "We earn a commission on every booking made through the agent." One sentence. Then: average group trip spend, projected take rate, unit economics path. | Airbnb's one-sentence model |
| 9 | **Traction & Conviction Signals** | What we have: working iMessage agent (demo it live), mobile app, voice calls, booking pipeline in progress. Customer interview quotes. Waitlist / early user signals. If metrics exist, show them. If not, show the Jennifer Test: "Would Jennifer, a non-technical 28-year-old, text this agent to plan her bachelorette trip?" | Airbnb's testimonials, Superhuman's design artifacts |
| 10 | **Competition** | 2x2 positioning chart. X-axis: Manual ↔ AI-Powered. Y-axis: Standalone App ↔ Lives in Group Chat. Tryps in top-right (AI + group chat native). TripIt, Wanderlog, Google Travel = manual + standalone. ChatGPT = AI but not group-native. Group chats = group-native but no AI. | Airbnb's 2x2 competition quadrant |
| 11 | **Team** | Jake (founder, MIT fall 2026), Asif (lead dev, iMessage + voice), Nadeem (app + UX), Rizwan (agent intelligence + booking). Marty (AI agent running 24/7). 11 people total. Advisors if notable. | DocSend: team = #1 time spent by investors |
| 12 | **The Ask** | How much you're raising, what it funds (complete booking pipeline, launch, first 1,000 trips), 12-month milestones. End with: "Text +1 917 745 3624 to try it right now." | Airbnb's explicit ask + Wispr's "product is the pitch" |

### Slide-by-Slide Design Notes

- **Slide 1:** Stark. White background. One sentence. The tagline does all the work.
- **Slide 2:** Real screenshots, not illustrations. Show the actual chaos of a group chat. Make investors feel the pain.
- **Slide 3:** The "aha" moment. This is where the concept clicks. Keep it to one core insight.
- **Slide 4:** This is the money slide. If possible, do a LIVE DEMO instead of static screenshots. Text the agent during the pitch. Wispr's lesson: make investors experience the product, not just hear about it.
- **Slide 9:** If pre-launch with minimal metrics, follow Airbnb's playbook: customer quotes, waitlist numbers, behavioral proof that demand exists. Never show a weak traction slide — omit it and replace with conviction signals (YC advice).
- **Slide 12:** End with the phone number. Let investors text the agent right there. This is your Wispr move — make investors into users in the room.

### The "No Deck" Play (Supplementary Strategy)

Based on Wispr Flow and Partiful's playbooks, consider a parallel strategy:

1. **Before any pitch meeting:** Text the Tryps agent number to target investors. Say "I'm planning a trip with some friends, try this." Let them experience it.
2. **During the pitch:** Don't present slides. Say "You texted our agent last week. That's the product. Here's where we're going."
3. **The deck becomes a leave-behind**, not the presentation itself.

This is the highest-conviction play. It only works if the demo is genuinely impressive. But if it is, nothing beats an investor who's already a user.
