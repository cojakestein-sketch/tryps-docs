The Concept

A game show host — dark suit, red tie, slicked hair, full Bob Barker / Pat Sajak energy — delivers a giveaway announcement like it's a real game show. A prize model walks across with an oversized "FREE TRYP" check. The host explains the entry steps. Then the custom TRYPS PRIZE WHEEL is revealed. He spins it — and the video cuts to black before it lands.

The old man IS the Tryps giveaway guy. No Jake. No founder. Just this character delivering the news like he's been hosting game shows his entire life. The audience came for the old man. They leave knowing Tryps.

Why it works: Nobody expects an old game show host on Instagram in 2026. The format is a pattern interrupt — it looks and sounds like nothing else in anyone's feed. The wheel is a game show prop, so the character justifies the format. And an old man speaking slowly with gravitas about a free trip is inherently funnier than a founder announcing his own giveaway.

Why the Cliffhanger Works

Cutting before the wheel lands: (1) creates rewatchability — people watch again to see where it was about to stop, (2) drives comments — "where did it land??" and (3) makes the viewer imagine THEIR destination, triggering the entry impulse.

Guardrails

1. The host plays it STRAIGHT. This is not a parody. He delivers it like a real game show. The comedy comes from the sincerity, not from winking at the camera.
2. No numbered checklist graphics for entry steps. One step per wheel spin. Appearing and disappearing.
3. No "good luck" or "winner announced on..." — logistics go in the caption, not the video.
4. No royalty-free music. The wheel clicks are the sound design. If music, it's a low dramatic tone underneath — game show tension, not ukulele.
5. No Jake in this video. The host IS the Tryps giveaway character. One voice, one energy, all the way through.

Production

The Set

White backdrop. Not green screen. Green screen is a trap — bad edges on the suit lapels, color spill on the red tie, the check reflecting green. White backdrop says "we chose this." It makes every color pop — the red tie, the Tryps branding, the red/white wheel.

  • Seamless white paper running down the wall AND across the floor — no visible seam where wall meets ground. The host should appear to be standing in an infinite white space, not in front of a white wall.
  • Custom Tryps prize wheel on a stand, positioned OFF-FRAME-RIGHT during beats 1-3. Not visible until the reveal at 0:18.
  • Optional: subtle red gel on a floor light aimed at the backdrop — introduces Tryps brand color into the environment without adding a "set." Barely perceptible warm red glow on the lower portion of the white background.

Camera — Three Setups

Setup A — The Master (0:00-0:09, intercut during 0:09-0:18)

  • 35mm lens at T2.8 (~50mm full-frame equivalent on FX6 Super 35)
  • Host positioned frame-right at a rule-of-thirds line. Open negative space frame-left for the model to walk through.
  • Camera at chest height, an inch or two below eye level — subtle power angle.
  • Locked on sticks. No movement. Stillness = game show = premium.

Setup B — Medium Close-Up (intercut during 0:09-0:18)

  • 85mm at T2. Shallow depth of field — white backdrop goes completely soft.
  • Host from mid-chest up. This is the "delivery" shot for entry steps.
  • Slightly off-axis — host looking 5 degrees past the lens, as if addressing a studio audience. Not dead-to-lens (that reads as corporate video).
  • Optional: barely perceptible 2-inch push-in on a slider over the 9-second duration. Subliminal.

The 85mm shallow-focus shots are the $50K look for $500. When the background goes creamy and the host's eye is tack-sharp, it reads as cinema.

Setup C — The Wheel (0:18-0:25)

  • 35mm wide for the reveal (host + full wheel in frame)
  • 85mm close-up of the wheel spinning — tight enough to read destination names clicking past the flapper. Shallow DOF so only the flapper section is sharp.
  • The reveal: host walks toward the wheel (exits master frame). CUT to Setup C wide reveals host arriving at wheel. The cut IS the reveal.

Lighting

  • Key: Aputure 600d (or equiv) through 4x4 silk, 45 degrees camera-left, 2ft above eye line. Rembrandt key — clear shadow on camera-right cheek. Broadcast lighting energy.
  • Fill: 4x4 bead board (white side) camera-right. Passive bounce, not powered. Shadows ~2 stops under key. No crossed shadows.
  • Rim: Aputure 300x with 10-degree grid, high and behind host camera-right. Rims the dark suit shoulder and hair edge. Separates host from white backdrop.
  • Background: Two lights on the white backdrop, evenly spread, blown to pure white (~2 stops over exposure). If underlit, it goes grey and reads cheap.
  • Accent: Small hard source gelled RED on the floor behind host, aimed at backdrop. Subtle red glow on lower portion of white background. Introduces Tryps brand color. Second red accent near the wheel for the reveal.
  • Wheel: Dedicated hard light with barn doors, 30 degrees above. Gives the wheel its own "glamour light" — flapper catches highlights, destination names are legible.

Audio

  • No music. Music makes this feel like an ad. The absence of music makes it feel like television.
  • Primary: Boom mic (Sennheiser MKH 50 or similar shotgun) just out of frame above. More natural, present sound with room character.
  • Safety: Lav hidden under the host's tie.
  • Wheel: Separate small shotgun or contact mic near the flapper for clean clicks. In post, layer a pitched-down version underneath for weight. Clicks should be loudest thing in the mix from 0:22 onward.
  • Room tone: Record 30 seconds of silence on set. This becomes the audio bed — gives the space "size."
  • Post: Short plate reverb on host's voice (0.4-0.6s decay). Think late-night talk show desk, not cathedral.
  • Model footsteps: If she's in heels on a hard floor, keep the clicks. If on seamless paper, Foley heel clicks in post.
  • Subtle crowd murmur: Layer a barely audible small studio audience reaction at two moments: (1) model walks in with check, (2) wheel is revealed. 15-20 dB below dialogue. Subliminal "this is a show" energy. If you notice it, it's too loud.
  • Cut to black: Audio dies HARD with the picture. No fade. One frame clicking, next frame total digital silence. Hold silence 1 second. Then a soft "ding" on the logo reveal.

The Cut to Black — Edit Sequence

  • 0:22.0 — Wide shot: wheel spinning, host watching, hand just released. (0.5s)
  • 0:22.5 — Tight close-up of wheel. Flapper clicking. Names blurring. Starting to decelerate. (1.5s)
  • 0:24.0 — Host's face, medium close-up. Watching the wheel. Slight grin. He knows something. (0.5s)
  • 0:24.5 — Back to tight wheel. Slowing more. Clicks spacing out. Almost readable... (0.5s)
  • 0:25.0 — HARD CUT TO BLACK. No fade. No dissolve. Absolute silence.
  • Hold black 1 second. Tryps logo fades up (0.5s). Soft "ding." CTA text. Hold 2 seconds.

The Make-or-Break Decision

Critical

The host is everything. The suit, the set, the wheel are costumes and props. They only work if the person wearing them inhabits the role. A professional actor with hosting experience makes a $500 shoot look like $50K. A founder's buddy who's "pretty comfortable on camera" makes a $50K shoot look like a TikTok skit. Cast well. That's the whole ballgame.
