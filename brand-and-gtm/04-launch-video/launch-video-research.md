## Part 2: Best Practices Synthesis

### 5 Non-Negotiable Rules

**1. Hook in 3 seconds, demo by 10.**
The scroll is ruthless. Your first frame needs to stop someone mid-thumb. Show the product working or present a question/tension that demands resolution. Do not open with a logo animation, a company name, or "Hi, I'm Jake." Open with the product doing something or a line that creates a gap in the viewer's brain.

**2. Show, don't tell. Under 60 seconds for social, under 90 for everything else.**
Loom did it in 42 seconds. Wispr Flow's viral clips were under 2 minutes. Dropbox proved demand with a 3-minute demo. For social cuts (TikTok, Reels, Shorts), 15-45 seconds is the sweet spot. For a hero launch video (YouTube, website), 60-90 seconds max. Every second must earn its place. If a section doesn't make someone think "I need this" or "I need to send this to someone," cut it.

**3. One emotional trigger, one distribution mechanic.**
Every viral launch married one feeling (FOMO, laughter, pride, surprise) to one built-in sharing mechanic (referral reward, watermark, invite system, shareable output). For Tryps: the emotional trigger is "my friend group actually planned a trip" and the distribution mechanic is the shared itinerary link itself.

**4. The founder on camera builds trust (but only if they're real).**
Dollar Shave Club, Rabbit R1, Wispr Flow, Arc Browser. The founder's face and voice signal "a real person built this and stands behind it." But it only works if they're genuine. A scripted, teleprompter-reading founder is worse than no founder at all. Jake should be on camera if (and only if) he can be conversational and unpolished.

**5. Every claim must be bulletproof.**
Humane showed pre-release software that gave wrong answers. The internet destroyed them. If your launch video says "plan a trip in 30 seconds," it better take 30 seconds. If it says "everyone in the group chat said yes," show a real group chat. Never give the audience a reason to call BS.

### Common Mistakes That Kill Launch Videos

**Opening with your logo or company name.** Nobody cares about your brand yet. They care about their problem.

**Listing features instead of showing outcomes.** "We have collaborative itinerary building, real-time cost splitting, and AI-powered suggestions" means nothing. Show a group chat where five friends go from "we should do something this summer" to "flights booked, Airbnb confirmed, activities planned" in 30 seconds.

**Over-produced corporate polish.** A16z's research confirms: "No one gives a shit about a cinematic launch video. Make videos that make people think 'these guys are going to make so much money.'" The iPhone + ring light Wispr video outperformed million-dollar productions.

**Making a video only your team would share.** The gut-check before publishing: "Would a stranger send this to a friend?" If the answer isn't obviously yes, rework it. Congratulatory comments from your network ("congrats on the launch!") are the kiss of death. Real traction looks like strangers tagging friends.

**Trying to explain everything.** One video, one idea. Dropbox showed file syncing. Dollar Shave Club showed cheap razors delivered monthly. Loom showed videos turning into docs. If you can't describe what the video proves in one sentence, it's too complex.

**Forgetting platform-native behavior.** A horizontal YouTube video reposted to TikTok with black bars is dead on arrival. Each platform has different pacing, aspect ratios, and audience expectations. You need platform-specific cuts, not reposts.

**Treating the launch as the finish line.** As Nico Christie (Fundamental) puts it: "A launch is a weapon, not the goal." The video gets attention. What you do with that attention in the first 30-60 minutes decides everything.

### The Formula: What the Best Ones Share Structurally

**The 5-Beat Structure:**

1. **The Hook (0-3 seconds):** A visual or verbal pattern interrupt. The most intense, confusing, or satisfying moment. Frame 1 earns frame 2.
   - Wispr: "If you can find ONE mistake, I'll give you my Porsche"
   - Dollar Shave Club: "For $1 a month, we send high-quality razors right to your door"
   - Dropbox: Screen recording of a file magically syncing

2. **The Problem (3-8 seconds):** Name the pain your audience already feels. Don't educate them on a problem they don't know about. Validate what they already hate.
   - "Planning a group trip is a nightmare"
   - "Your group chat has 47 unread messages and nobody knows what hotel you're staying at"

3. **The Product in Action (8-40 seconds):** Show the product solving the problem in real time. This is the bulk of the video. No narration of features, just the product working. Real screens, real taps, real results. Micro-payoffs every 10-15 seconds (something satisfying happens on screen).

4. **The Social Proof / Stakes (40-50 seconds):** A testimonial, a stat, or a moment that makes the viewer think "other people already want this." Can be as simple as showing a waitlist number or a friend's reaction.

5. **The CTA (last 5-10 seconds):** One action. Download, sign up, join the waitlist. Not "follow us on all platforms and sign up for our newsletter and tell your friends." One thing.

### Platform-Specific Cuts

**TikTok (15-45 seconds, 9:16 vertical)**
- Raw, fast-paced, native-feeling. Should look like it belongs on TikTok, not like an ad that landed there.
- Use trending sounds or original audio that feels organic.
- Hook must land in frame 1. TikTok users decide to stay or leave faster than any other platform.
- Caption text on screen (not just in the caption field). Many watch without sound.
- Best format for Tryps: "POV: your friend group actually booked the trip" with screen recording of the app.
- 15-30 seconds for maximum performance.

**Instagram Reels (15-30 seconds, 9:16 vertical)**
- Slightly more polished than TikTok. Instagram rewards slick, snappy editing.
- Longer descriptive captions with 5-10 strategic hashtags.
- Same vertical format but the audience expects a bit more production value.
- Best format for Tryps: Lifestyle-forward. Show the trip happening, then reveal "we planned this in 5 minutes on Tryps."

**YouTube (60-90 seconds, 16:9 horizontal OR 9:16 Shorts)**
- Two cuts: a 60-second Short (vertical) and a 60-90 second hero video (horizontal).
- YouTube allows more storytelling. You can take 5 seconds to set up the problem before showing the product.
- Search-optimized title and description matter here. People find YouTube videos through search, not just feed.
- Best format for Tryps: "How 6 friends planned a trip to Mexico in under 2 minutes" with full walkthrough.

**X / Twitter (30-60 seconds, 16:9 horizontal or 1:1 square)**
- The first 30-60 minutes of engagement on X decide whether a launch video takes off.
- Quote tweets are the viral mechanic. Make the video easy to react to.
- Pair the video with a text hook in the tweet itself: "We spent 2 years building this. Here's what group trip planning should look like."
- Prepare a "champion list" of 20-50 people to send the tweet to in the first 10 minutes.
- Best format for Tryps: Product demo with founder voiceover, tweet copy that creates curiosity.

**Product Hunt (45-90 seconds, embedded)**
- 45-90 seconds is the sweet spot. Shorter feels incomplete, longer loses attention.
- Show the product in action immediately. No logo intros.
- Schedule post for 00:01 San Francisco time to capture global upvotes.
- Secure a respected hunter who genuinely loves the product.

## Part 3: Framework for Review Session

When Jake and Sean watch each of the 20 videos together, answer these three questions for every single one:

### Question 1: What worked?

What specific moment made you stop scrolling or lean in? Was it a visual, a line of copy, the pacing, the music, the founder's energy? Write down the exact timestamp where you felt it. That's your reference point. If nothing made you feel anything, note that too, because that's data.

### Question 2: What didn't work?

Where did you lose interest or cringe? Was it too long? Too corporate? Did it feel like an ad? Did the founder seem fake? Was the demo confusing? Did you stop believing them? Be honest and specific. "It felt off" is not useful. "At 0:22 they started listing features and I checked out" is useful.

### Question 3: What can we pull for Tryps?

This is the action question. For each video, identify one concrete thing you could adapt for the Tryps launch. It could be a format ("founder challenge video like Wispr"), a structural choice ("hook with the outcome like Loom"), a distribution tactic ("haptic onboarding like Poparazzi"), or even a single frame composition. Write it as a directive: "Tryps should do X because Y."

## Bottom Line for Tryps

The launches that worked best share three traits:

1. **The product is the star.** Not the founder's story, not the brand, not the production value. The product doing something that makes you say "I need that."

2. **The format matches the feeling.** A group trip planning app should feel fun, social, and slightly chaotic (like actual group trip planning). It should not feel corporate, polished, or tech-startup-y. The video should look like something a friend would send in a group chat, not something a marketing team would present in a boardroom.

3. **The launch mechanic is built into the product.** Every trip planned on Tryps is a Tryps ad. Every shared itinerary is a referral. Every group chat link is distribution. The video gets the first wave of attention. The product keeps it going.

Jake's advantage: he's building something people genuinely want (group trip planning is a universal pain point) and he's got a built-in distribution mechanic (every trip invite is sent to 4-10 friends). The launch video just needs to capture that energy and get out of the way.
