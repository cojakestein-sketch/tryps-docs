## 5. Valuation / Terms Expectations

### Recommended Structure: Post-Money SAFE

**Why SAFE over priced round:** Pre-seed rounds in 2025-2026 almost universally use SAFEs (Simple Agreement for Future Equity). They avoid the legal cost of a priced round ($25K-$50K in legal fees), close faster, and defer valuation negotiation to the Seed round when there's real data to price against.

**Why post-money (not pre-money):** Post-money SAFEs (the YC standard) make dilution math simple. If you raise $1.5M on a $7.5M post-money cap, investors own exactly 20%. No ambiguity. This is the market standard.

### Valuation Cap Range

| Factor | Tryps | Impact on Valuation |
|--------|-------|---------------------|
| Stage | Pre-revenue, pre-launch | Pulls down |
| Demo | Working end-to-end (iMessage agent, app, voice, booking in progress) | Pushes up |
| Team | 11 people, mostly contractors, no co-founder | Neutral to pulls down |
| Market | Travel + social — large TAM, proven category | Pushes up |
| Comparable | Partiful raised $20M Series A1 at $140M for events (adjacent category) | Pushes up |
| Founder | Solo founder, first-time, going to MIT | Neutral |
| Location | NYC | Neutral (NYC pre-seed ecosystem is strong) |

**Recommended valuation cap: $6M-$8M post-money**

| Cap | Investor Ownership at $1.5M | Notes |
|-----|----------------------------|-------|
| $6M | 25% | Aggressive for investors. Appropriate if there's competitive interest and strong demo. |
| $7.5M | 20% | Sweet spot. Standard for a pre-seed with a working product and a real team. |
| $8M | 18.75% | Justified if there's heat (multiple term sheets, strong lead). |
| $10M | 15% | Too high for pre-revenue. Would require exceptional traction or a brand-name lead. |

**The argument for $7.5M post-money ($6M pre-money):**
- The product works. This is not a slide deck — there's a functioning iMessage agent, a mobile app, voice calls, and flight booking infrastructure.
- The team is 11 people shipping. Most pre-seed companies are 2 people with a prototype.
- The market is massive. Group travel is a $900B+ market (US leisure travel alone). Partiful proved the "social coordination" category commands premium valuations.
- The viral mechanic is inherent. Every trip invite is a user acquisition event. This is not theoretical — the trip card and invite flow exist.

**Investor ownership expectation:** Pre-seed investors typically want 15-25% ownership. At $1.5M, a $7.5M cap giving 20% is right in the middle of that range.

### Pro Rata Rights

Expect the lead investor to request pro rata rights (the right to invest in future rounds to maintain their ownership percentage). This is standard and reasonable. Grant it.

### MFN (Most Favored Nation)

If you're doing multiple SAFE closings (rolling close), give early investors an MFN clause so they automatically get the best terms of any subsequent SAFE in this round.

## 7. Risks and What Kills You

### Risk 1: Solo Founder Fatigue (CRITICAL)

Jake is the only decision-maker, the only US-based team member, the only person with full context. Going to MIT in fall 2026 adds a second full-time commitment. The risk is that Jake burns out or gets pulled in too many directions, and nobody else can make product decisions.

**Mitigation:** Convert Cameron to Head of Product (if strong) or find a co-founder / head of product who can own the product while Jake is at MIT. The growth hire also takes execution load off Jake.

### Risk 2: Contractor Retention Without Enough Equity (HIGH)

The dev team in Pakistan has no equity and no formal employment relationship. If Asif gets a better offer tomorrow, the company loses its lead dev and institutional knowledge. This is the single biggest operational risk.

**Mitigation:** FTE conversion + equity grants for Asif and Nadeem (Priority 1 and 2 from the hire plan). Must happen within 30 days of funding.

### Risk 3: The Planning-to-Booking Gap (HIGH)

This is the existential product risk, well-documented in the pressure test. If users plan trips on Tryps but book on Expedia/Kayak/directly, revenue is zero. The entire revenue model depends on converting planning intent to booking action.

**Mitigation:** The iMessage agent's ability to say "I found flights — want me to book?" is the wedge. But this requires the booking flow to be frictionless. Payment info on file, one-tap confirmation, competitive prices. Engineering investment in the booking UX is critical months 1-6.

### Risk 4: Overspending on Marketing Before Product-Market Fit (MEDIUM)

The $15K/month marketing budget could be wasted if the invite flow isn't good enough (K < 0.2). Every dollar of marketing spend gets 1.2x leverage if K=0.2, but 1.67x if K=0.4. Spending before the viral loop works is literally throwing money away.

**Mitigation:** The growth plan from `path-to-100k-downloads.md` already accounts for this. Month 1-3 should be product-focused (nail the invite flow, measure K). Marketing scales in month 4+ only if K > 0.3. If K < 0.2, redirect marketing budget to engineering.

### Risk 5: LLM Cost Explosion (MEDIUM)

Every trip conversation involves multiple LLM calls. At 50K users with 2-4 trips each, that's 100K-200K trips/year. At $0.50-$3.00 per trip in LLM costs, that's $50K-$600K/year in API costs alone. This could eat the budget.

**Mitigation:** Model optimization (smaller models for routine tasks, Claude only for complex reasoning), caching common responses, batching API calls. Monitor LLM cost per trip as a key metric from day one.

### Risk 6: Linq Dependency (MEDIUM)

The iMessage integration depends on Linq's infrastructure and roadmap (native polling). If Linq goes down, pivots, or deprioritizes features Tryps needs, the core product is at risk.

**Mitigation:** Maintain a direct relationship with Linq leadership. Explore backup iMessage providers (SendBlue). The mobile app can function independently of iMessage — it's degraded but not dead.

### Risk 7: Apple App Store Rejection (LOW but CATASTROPHIC)

If Apple rejects the app or changes iMessage integration policies, the entire distribution thesis is at risk.

**Mitigation:** Submit early (April 15 target is good), maintain compliance with Apple guidelines, have a fallback launch plan via TestFlight while resolving any rejection issues.

### Risk 8: Spending $1.5M and Having Nothing to Show (THE REAL FAILURE MODE)

The failure mode is not running out of money — it's running out of money without learning anything. If at month 12, Tryps has spent $1.2M but has 3,000 downloads, K=0.1, and no revenue, the company is dead. Not because of the money, but because there's no story for the next round.

**What kills you:** Spending the first 6 months "perfecting the product" instead of getting it into users' hands. The biggest risk at pre-seed is not shipping fast enough and not learning from real users fast enough.

## 8. The Pitch for Why $1.5M is Right

### Why Not Less ($500K-$750K)

A smaller raise sounds capital-efficient but creates three problems:

1. **You can't convert anyone to FTE.** At $500K, you have 6-8 months of runway at current burn. There's no room for a founder salary, FTE conversions, or a growth hire. You're stuck in contractor mode with all the retention risk that implies.

2. **You can't fund growth.** The path to 100K downloads requires $150K-$250K in marketing spend. A $500K raise leaves $0 for marketing after covering team costs.

3. **You raise again in 6 months from a weaker position.** If you raise $500K now and come back in 6 months needing more, investors see a founder who underestimated their needs. It's better to raise once and execute.

### Why Not More ($2M-$3M)

A larger raise sounds like more runway but creates three problems:

1. **Higher valuation pressure.** To raise $2.5M at reasonable dilution (20%), you need a $12.5M post-money cap. That's aggressive for pre-revenue. You'll either take too much dilution or spend months negotiating a valuation you can't justify.

2. **Higher expectations.** Raising $2.5M at pre-seed sets Series A expectations at $15M-$25M+ valuation. That requires 200K+ users and meaningful revenue. Missing those targets means a down round or death.

3. **Discipline erosion.** With $2.5M in the bank, the temptation is to hire a 6th dev, a head of marketing, a VP of BD, and a data scientist. A 15-person team at pre-seed is a company that's spending to feel like a company instead of spending to find product-market fit.

### Why $1.5M is the Goldilocks Amount

**$1.5M gives Tryps exactly enough to answer three questions:**

1. **Does the product work?** (Launch on App Store, get to 500+ users, measure engagement.)
2. **Does the viral loop work?** (Measure K-factor with real data. Is every user bringing in 0.4+ new users?)
3. **Does the revenue model work?** (Are people booking through Tryps, or just planning on it and booking elsewhere?)

If the answer to all three is yes, Tryps raises a $5-8M Seed with strong data. If any answer is no, the remaining runway gives time to iterate before the money runs out.

**The math is clean:**
- $1.5M / $95K monthly burn = ~16 months of runway
- 16 months = launch (month 1) + growth experiment (months 2-6) + scale what works (months 7-12) + 4-month buffer to raise the Seed
- The buffer matters. Seed rounds take 2-4 months. You need to start raising at month 10-12, not month 14 when you're out of cash.

**$1.5M is also a round size that pre-seed VCs understand.** It's not so small that it signals lack of ambition, and not so large that it requires a big-name lead. A $500K-$750K lead from a pre-seed fund plus $750K-$1M from angels is a very achievable round composition.

### The MIT Angle

Jake going to MIT in fall 2026 is a feature, not a bug:

- **MIT network:** Martin Trust Center, MIT Angels, Boston VC ecosystem, Sloan connections
- **Credibility signal:** MIT acceptance validates the founder
- **Forcing function:** Jake has to build a team that can operate without him in every meeting. This forces real delegation and organizational maturity.
- **Fundraising:** MIT Demo Day and the broader MIT entrepreneurship ecosystem provide warm intro paths to Seed investors

The $1.5M pre-seed funds the transition: by the time Jake starts at MIT, the team is FTE, the product is launched, growth is measurable, and there's a Head of Product or co-founder running day-to-day.

### The Bottom Line

$1.5M buys 14-16 months to go from "this is a really compelling demo" to "this is a real company with real users, real revenue, and a proven growth engine." That's the entire job of a pre-seed round.
